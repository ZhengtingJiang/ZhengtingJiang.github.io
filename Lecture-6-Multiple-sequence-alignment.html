Lecture 6 Multiple sequence alignment
The idea behind the multiple sequence alignment: We take three or more related sequences and
align them so that the greatest number of similar characters are aligned in the same column of
the alignment. However, an multiple sequence alignment is feasible if and only if it satisfies the
following two requirements:
1. Order requirement: Ignoring the gap characters '-', the row associated with a sequences
must be identical to sequence , and preserve the order of its characters
.
2. Column alignment requirement: Each set of aligned characters belongs to exactly one
column, and any column must contain at east one character .
A feasible alignment is optimal if it gives the highest objective value with respect to the scoring
function.
6.1 Information content of a multiple alignment
Sequences can be conserved across species and perform similar or identical functions, so
sequences hold information about which regions have high mutation rates over evolutionary time
and which are evolutionarily conserved, besides, the identification of regions or domains that are
critical to function can be deduced by sequence alignment. Sequences can be mutated or
rearranged to perform an altered function. So the changes in the coding region may cause a
change in function.

What is a heuristic algorithm? A heuristic algorithm is a procedure that determines near-
optimal solutions to an optimization problem. It does not guarantee that an optimal solution
will be found (especially for global optimal), but it always reaches a likely optimal result (local
optimal), typically, a heuristic is used to make an algorithm faster or sometimes makes solving a
problem feasible. Some problems have an exponential complexity, this relates to so-called NP-
complete problems in CS. NP stands for Non-Polynomial, meaning that the problem grows faster
than any fixed exponent.
What is an exact algorithm? An exact algorithms are algorithms that always solve an
optimization problem to optimality. It examines all possible aligned positions simultaneously,
and look for the optimal solution, however, it is very very slow.

The complexity will be if there are sequences with lengths of . It is impractical for even a
small number of short sequences. Lipman introduced a new method in 1989. The key idea is to
restrict the computational costs by determining a minimal region within the n-dimensional space
that will contain the optimal path.
Let us talk about it in detail: Let us consider 3 sequences. We calculate all pair-wise alignment
scores by Dynamic Programming. Next we use the scores to predict a tree. Then we produce a
heuristic multiple alignment based on the tree (quick and dirty), calculate lower bound score for
each sequence pair from this multiple alignment and determine paths with scores greater than
the lower bound score. Finally, we determine spatial positions in multidimentional matrix that
must be calculated to obtain the optimal alignment.
Notwithstanding the complexity optimization by means of bound determination, the MSA
method is only able to align 7-9 sequences of moderate length, this renders the method
infeasible for biological data sizes.
Later, Stoye introduced the divide and conquer approach. The idea is: each sequence is cut in two
behind a suitable cut position somewhere close to its midpoint, this way, the problem of aligning
one family of long sequences is divided into the two problems of aligning two families of shorter
sequences. This procedure is re-iterated until the sequences are sufficiently short. then we get the
optimal alignment by MSA. Finally, the resulting short alignments are concatenated.

families of sequences that are evolutionary related, so we have a principle: construct an

6.4 Many progressive alignment methods
Here we talk about the methods of progressive alignment.
General progressive multiple alignment technique (following generated tree)
However, there are problems with the progressive strategy, first, we have to address that the
accuracy is very important! Alignment errors during the construction of the MSA cannot repaired
anymore: errors become propagated during the progressive steps. The comparisons of sequences
at early steps during progressive alignments cannot make use of information from other
alignments. It is only later during the alignment progression that more information from other
sequences (e.g. through profile representation) becomes employed in the alignment steps. Lastly,
the progressive multiple alignment strategy is a greedy algorithm, things that are done (i.e.
sequence that have been aligned) are not reconsidered or changed anymore! So 'once a gap,
always a gap'.
Challenges for the progressive alignment strategy:
Alignment error increases non-linearity with evolutionary sequence distance (sequence
similarity).
'Once a gap always a gap' - progressive alignment is greedy (no reconsideration of affected
alignments)
Error propagation because of greedy nature of progressive alignment - early errors largest
problem.
Sequence information not used optimally - only at the very end of the progressive protocol
(when it is too late) is information from all sequences included.
So we could give some tricks for progressive alignment optimization:

6.5 Iterative alignment
It corrects for problems with progressive alignment by repeatedly realigning (subgroups of) the
sequence.
The idea behind the algorithm is an optimal alignment might be found by repeatedly modifying
and optimizing existing suboptimal solutions. This is sometimes called bootstrapping: learn from
what you have done and then do it again in a better way. It starts with producing low quality
alignment and gradually improve by iterative refinement, and continues until no more
improvements in the alignment scores can be achieve.

We here talk about the consistency iteration. MSA consistency may be defined using the
relationship between the all-against-all pairwise alignments and a MSA derived from these. The
'best' MSA therefore would be the one that is most consistent when compared to the pairwise
alignments.
6.6 Benchmarking multiple sequence alignment method
How can we benchmark multiple sequence alignment methods? How to find out which method is
best? We will use reference (or benchmark) alignments for this (gold standard alignments), there
are databases for this. Having a number of such benchmark alignments we calculate a MSA of the
input sequences using an alignment method and look to what extent each calculated alignment
looks like the reference alignment. We do this for multiple methods and then compare the results.
We could use scoring a single MSA with the sum-of-pairs (SP) score. The Sum-of-Pairs score
calculates the sum of all pairwise alignment scores (all-against-all pairs of sequences), good
alignments should have a high SP score, but it is not always the case that the true biological
alignment has the highest score. We could see the graph .
The calculation is equivalent to taking the sum of all matched amino acid pairs (red lines). The
latter can be done using gap penalties or not. The SP score is the score obtained by pairwise
dynamic programming.
We have evaluation measures with gold standard. We have column score and sum-of-pairs score.
6.7 Evaluating multiple alignments. Charlie Chaplin problem.
Many BALiBASE reference alignments have a lower SP score (scoring a single MSA) than the
corresponding alignment obtained by PRALINE (ran in bock standard mode without any
optimization).
And here you see that for larger BALiBASE reference alignments, more than half have the PROLINE
standard alignment scoring a higher SP score than the corresponding BALiBASE reference
alignment.
So how good is the SP score as an objective function (OF)? Not any better than the OF of the jury
during the Charlie Chaplin contest in which Charlie himself took part and became third. The
conclusion: biological appropariateness has not been reached with the current scoring schemes.
