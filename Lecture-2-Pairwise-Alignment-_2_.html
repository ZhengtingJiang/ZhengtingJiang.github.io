2.5 Local Alignment
Let us summarize these three types of alignments:
Global alignment: align all residues in both sequences, all gaps are penalized.
Semi-global alignment: align all residues in both sequences; end gaps are not penalized (zero
end gap penalties).
Local alignment, which we will talk later: align one fragment of each sequence; end gaps are
not applicable. It can be used in the dynamic programming, but end-gaps are never
displayed.
The word 'local' means that it allows only parts of the sequence to match, and results in high
scoring segments. Locally maximal refers to the it cannot be better by trimming or extending the
alignment. We use the local alignment is for the purposes that: evolutionary pressure does not put
constraints on the whole sequence, leading to parts of the sequence diverge faster; proteins have
modular construction, that is, perhaps they have some identical modular construction, which
share domains between sequences.
We take the immunoglobulin domain as an example.

2.7 Pitfalls of alignment
Alignments need to be able to skip secondary structural elements to complete domains. (i.e.
putting gaps opposite these motifs in the shorter sequence)
Gap situations can be even more complex in cases of alternatively spliced protein variants.
Depending on gap penalties chosen, the algorithm might have difficulty with making such
long gaps (e.g. when using high affine gap penalties), resulting in incorrect alignments
Alignments are only meaningful for homologous sequences having a common ancestor.
