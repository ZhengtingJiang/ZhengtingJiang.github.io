4.1 The Human Genome Project (HGP)
DNA sequence is a long string, to determine what exactly they are, people developed some
methods to measure them, particularly, the human genome project was set to establish a global
project to determine the sequence of human genome. The project used traditional sequencing
techniques, i.e. the Sanger sequencing. The project was initiated in 1993 but during the
experiment time, there is a new man called Dr. Craig Venter who owns a company called Celera
Genomics, they used a method called 'shotgun method' compared with the HGP. The method is
much more smarter than HGP, due to some factors, they published the result together. Here we
introduce the two methods.
Shotgun: (From Bottom to Up)
For any genomic DNA, first they generate many short fragments which are clones, and than
sequence each clone, finally, assemble sequence by aligning and removing overlaps.
Sanger sequencing: (From Top to Down)
For any genomic DNA, first generate long fragments and BAC clone library, which are ordered
to form a complete physical map, next generate a sub-clone library from each BAC clone,
then they sequence each subclone, finally, assemble sequence by aligning and removing
overlaps.
In shortgun sequencing, DNA is broken up randomly into numerous small segments, which are
sequenced using the chain termination method to obtain reads. Multiple overlapping reads for
the target DNA are obtained by performing several rounds of this fragmentation and sequencing.
Computer programs then use the overlapping ends of different reads to assemble them into
blocks of contiguous sequence (contigs).
In top-down mapping a single chromosome is cut into large pieces (chunks of around 150kb). The
chunks are carefully mapped such that the exact location of each chunk is known. The chunks are
then cut up again -- each piece is again 'shotgunned' into 2kb fragments and sequenced. The
resulting map depicts the order and distance between the original cleaves.
There are 23 chromosome pairs and more than 20000 genes, about 2.9 of 3.3 billion base pairs
sequences, however, easily more than half of the human genome comprises repetitive elements.
This is a challenge for reconstruction algorithm.
4.2 Next Generation Sequencing (NGS)
Scientists developed another method to sequence DNA. It is NGS. Massively paralled sequencing
of millions to billions of short fragments and it is very fast, for example, compared to Sanger
sequencing - exploited in HGP -max 384 DNA samples in a single batch in up to 24 runs a day, now
huge amounts of data generated in single sequencing experiment, many TBs, and the cost was
reduced, for 1 human genome, it takes 3 billion for HGP but only 10000 for NGS. Lastly, shorter
fragments (reads) are obtained than Sanger sequencing, many different techniques exist but
based on approx. same. Differences reside mainly in chemical usage and the way fragments are
stuck to the surface.
As we introduced before, the problem is the sequencing or assembly. Reconstructing a DNA
sequence from many randomly selected short fragments (reads) is difficult, because reads may
contain experimental errors. So we have to put the reads together using bioinformatics, two main
ways of stringing together the very many small fragments into a complete genome sequence:
De novo assembly of a genome
Assembly using alignment onto a reference genome.

Reconstructing a complete genome de novo requires testing possible overlaps between all reads

A known problem in computer science is the shortest superstring problem, where all fragments
are strung up to produce the shortest over string (i.e. genome). However, the shortest possible
string is not an ideal criterion because genomes have many repeating fragments. We have to
introduce a word: contig. Computer programs have to check the overlap of all against all
fragments to find the most likely order of the fragments, resulting in a completely reconstructed
DNA sequence.

However, there are two main problems: the first is there are multiple contigs (due to lack of

Lack of coverage.
When we use some sequencing methods to get the reads, it might cause 'lack of coverage'.
Lack of coverage of the reads on the original genome causes multiple contigs, because
randomness of shearing process there is a chance that some regions of the genome are
unsequenced.
A natural question is: how to deal with gaps in between contigs? If a number of unconnected
contigs are present after building them using overlapping reads, the order of the contigs in the
genome cannot be determined. A way to alleviate this problem is by using paired-end reads or
mate pairs. These are longer fragments of known length where only the short flanking regions are
sequenced (i.e. the number of nucleotides in between the sequenced franks in known).
4.4 Paired-end Reads and Mate Pairs
High-molecular-weight DNA is sheared into random fragments, size-selected (usually 2, 5, 10 kb).
Fragments are sequenced from both ends, yielding two short sequences, each sequence is called
an end-read and two reads bridging a fragment are referred to as mate pairs or (paired-end reads).
Because fragments are size-selected, the distance between the two reads is known. Celera
basically used paired-end reads, much more than HGC.
We would like to talk about the difference between the paired-end and the mate pairs.
Length difference:
Paired-end: Each 'read' is two sequences a pair from each end of the same genomic DNA
fragments. The distance between the reads on the original genome sequence (150-800
bp depending on NGS technique) is roughly equal to the length of the fragment that was
sequenced at either end (minus the length of the ends.)
Mate-pair: Like paired-end reads, each 'read' is two sequences from each end of the
same DNA fragment, but the distance between the reads on the original genome
sequence is much longer (3000-10000bp) than a single read (200-800bp). To get these
longer 'in-between' fragments, the trick here is that DNA fragment has been engineered
from a circularization process.
NGS fragments are shorter than Sanger fragments, paired-end reads bridge shorter fragments
than mate pairs.
Next we have to combine contigs with paired-end reads:
First we align paired-end reads to contigs.
Combine contigs (into scaffolds, so contig order is know) if linked by paired ends, may be
conditional on:
Number of bridges
Alignment accuracy
Uniqueness of alignment (information content).
Use insert size to estimate gap between the contigs.
However, as we mentioned before, repetitive elements can cause major problems to the
assembler; Because reads corresponding to two separate repeats may be collapsed in a single
contig, and repeats with large intervening regions of multiple repeat regions (e.g. >600 repeats)
cannot be resolved by mate pairs anymore.
In additional to linking contigs, paired-end reads can also reveal repeating fragments. We take an
example of sequence assembly with or without applying forward-reverse constraint (mate-pairs or
paired-end reads), which fixes the sequence distance from both ends of a subclone. Without the
restraint, the red fragment is misassembled due to matches of repetitive element in the middle of
a fragment.
We mentioned a bad situation - lack of coverage, here we define the measure of coverage and the
depth of coverage. Usage of sequencing 'depth' and sequencing 'coverage'.
Sequencing depth: (average) number of reads per base (often on entire sequencing sample).
Coverage: average number of reads per base (often on specific region).
We cannot manually construct the contigs, we want to construct contigs automatically from short
reads, we need an algorithm to construct the most likely DNA sequence given the reads.
4.5 Carrying out de novo assemblt
We will use Bruijn graphs to make genome assemblt.
In fact, the idea is to establish a Eulerian Path. We have to talk about k-mer first: K-mers in
assembly algorithms, are used in many bioinformatics algorithms optimized for speed, e.g. 'words'
in the widely-used BLAST suite. Example: ACTGTTA contains 4-mers: ACTG, CTGT, TGTT, GTTA
In the de Bruijn Graph, nodes are k-mers, edges are (k+1)-mers, connecting two nodes, such nodes
need to have an overlap of (k-1) symbols, directed edges, such that the suffix of the outgoing node
overlaps with the prefix of the incoming node. The overlap
is 'TG'. TG is the suffix of ATG and the prefix of TGT.
Then we use reads to connect nodes, given a read for example, CAATGCC, 3-mers can be connect
if connecting 4-mers are present in the read. How to construct the de bruijn graph is introduced in
FoB.

4.7 Burrows-Wheel Aligner (BWA)
It is based on indexing technique using suffix tress formalism. It also can handle inexact repeats
with a defined maximum number of differences (mismatches or gaps). There is a paper we should
work on. BWA searches for an exact substring in time, where is the average read length.
For inexact searches (allowing up to k mismatches), the search times go up, but are kept under
control by defining lower bounds of the number of differences for each substring , this
limits the search space by avoiding searches across subtrees in the prefix trie.
BWA supports paired-end mapping: BWA first finds positions of all good hits (one end of paired-
end read), sorts them according to chromosomal coordinates and then does a linear scan through
all potential hits to pair the two ends. To calculate all the chromosomal coordinates requires
looking up the suffix array frequently, BWA performs Smith-Waterman alignment for unmapped
reads whose mates can be reliably aligned. Therefore, BWA employs SW alignment to rescue some
reads with excessive differences.
